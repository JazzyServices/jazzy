Puzzle 10

There is a pond with seven lily pads in a line that allow frogs to cross from one side of the pond to the other.
One day, three frogs approach from the West and want to cross to the East; meanwhile three frogs approach from the East and want to cross to the West.

Puzzle 10 step 1

As always, our first step is to eliminate impossibles. In the frog-jumping puzzle the impossible is for a frog to jump over two (or more) other frogs. Puzzle 10 step 2

It may be too difficult to find all of these situations that inevitably lead to an impossibility. So how do we proceed? A technique we can use is to solve a simpler puzzle! One way of doing this is to posit a situation from which we can easily solve the original puzzle then try to reach this intermediate situation from the starting position. So, if we could get the frogs into the following positions, we should be easily able to solve the puzzle.

Puzzle 10 step 4

Now, before finishing this puzzle, let us imagine that another eastbound frog and another westbound frog join the queue of frogs wishing to cross the pond
ewewew
We are only one move away (an ⓔ hop) from the position in step 2. A position from which we have already solved the puzzle. So, to reach the position in step 2, our sequence of moves would have been:
  • ⓔ hop
  • ⓦ jump, ⓦ hop
  • ⓔ jump, ⓔ jump, ⓔ hop
You should be able to see that it makes no difference if the two extra frogs joined the queue at the beginning of step 3 or the beginning of step 4.
You should also notice that there is a pattern to the sequence of moves. Each group makes a set of moves that always ends in a hop. Before the hop there are zero or more jumps. The number of jumps increases by one each time. In fact, what we have found is a sieve. This will allow is to solve the jumping frogs puzzle with any number of frogs.
