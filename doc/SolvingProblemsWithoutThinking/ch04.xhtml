Puzzle 4

Starting at zero...
  1. Continually add 3
  2. If you have a number greater than 10, subtract 10.
  3. If you get to exactly 10, stop.
If you have done it properly, you should have the sequence:
0 3 6 9 2 5 8 1 4 7 10
Is this a puzzle? It is so simple and mechanical that it is doesn’t involve any brain power. It is not puzzling.
This puzzle-solving technique is called a Sieve. It means, continually applying a simple set of steps until you arrive at the right answer.

The Two-Bucket Problem

You have a 4-gallon bucket and a 7-gallon bucket. Measure exactly 2 gallons.
You could probably solve this one after a short while. But what if you have a 33cl bottle and a 50cl bottle and you want exactly 25cl? Both problems are trivially the same, they just require a sieve
Starting at zero...
  1. Continually add 4 (that is, fill the 4-gallon bucket and tip it into the 7-gallon bucket)
  2. If you have a number greater than 7, subtract 7 (that is, if you fill the 7-gallon bucket, tip it away and pour in the rest of the 4-gallon bucket)
  3. If you get to exactly 2, stop.
To slow this down a bit ...
  1. Start with two empty buckets. We will represent this as the sum: 0+0
  2. Fill the smaller bucket. You now have 4+0
  3. Tip it into the bigger bucket. You have 0+4
  4. Fill the smaller bucket. You have 4+4
  5. Tip as much as you can (3 gallons) into the bigger bucket. You have 1+7
  6. Empty the bigger bucket. You have 1+0
  7. Tip the remainder into the bigger bucket. You have 0+1
  8. Fill the smaller bucket. You have 4+1
  9. Tip it into the bigger bucket. You have 0+5
  10. Fill the smaller bucket. You have 4+5
  11. Tip as much as you can (2 gallons) into the bigger bucket. You have 2+7
  12. Stop, because you have 2 gallons in the smaller bucket.
Now try it with the 33cl and 50cl bottles. It will take a few more steps, but it is just continually adding 33 and subtracting 50 when we go above it. Simple, and unpuzzling. And this is supposed to be a classic puzzle.
