Puzzle 2 part 1

Three light switches (left, middle and right) all start in the "off" position. You have to switch them all on. But the circuits are so dodgy that you have to obey certain rules otherwise you’ll cause a short-circuit:
  1. You can only flick one switch at a time (either on or off)
  2. You can’t switch the left one on leaving middle and right off.
  3. You can’t switch the right one on leaving middle and left off.
  4. If the left and middle switches are on, you can’t also turn the right switch on.
  5. If the right and middle switches are on, you can’t also turn the left switch on.

Lesson 2 - Draw a Map

Since we don’t have three light switches to hand we have to make a model, or map, of the puzzle to help us see the solution. We only have to represent two states for each switch — “on” and “off”, so we could use a binary notation. Zero for “off” and one for “on”. The switches all start in the off position, so that’s three zeroes.
000
In order to obey the first three rules there is only one thing we can do and that is flick the middle switch on.
000 010
We now have a choice. We can switch on either the left or the right switch. So on our map we will show both possibilities. (The third possibility is to turn the middle switch off again, which would take us back to the beginning – a state that is already on the map.)
000 010 110 011
Rule 4 now applies to the left-hand route. So we can’t turn on the right switch. Our only choice (apart from going backwards) is to turn off the middle switch. Similarly, rule 5 applies to the right-hand route. So we can’t turn on the left switch. Our only choice is to turn off the middle switch.
000 010 110 011 100 001
On the left-hand route we can only turn on the right switch. On the right-hand route we can only turn on the left switch. This brings us to the same point, whichever route we take.
000 010 110 011 100 001 101
And finally we can turn on the middle switch to complete the puzzle.
000 010 110 011 100 001 101 111

Puzzle 2 part 2

This way of mapping the puzzle is fine; but it is not the only way. Since we have three switches, we can represent them in 3-dimensional space. We could represent the left-hand switch on the x-axis (“on” moves to the right and “off” moves to the left), the middle switch on the y-axis (“on” moves up and “off” moves down) and the right-hand switch on the z-axis (“on” moves away and “off” moves towards).
Starting with the box that we used for Puzzle 1 part 2, apply the rules of this puzzle, which now become;
  1. You can only move along the edges.
  2. You can’t go right if you are down and toward.
  3. You can’t go away if you are down and left.
  4. If you are right and up, you can’t go away.
  5. If you are away and up, you can’t go right.
What you should discover is that the two puzzles are identical!
