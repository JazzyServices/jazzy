Puzzle 8

One puzzle that is a variant of the Latin Square puzzle is the futoshiki. Every row and every column must contain the numbers 1 to 5. The unique feature of the futoshiki is the “less than” and “greater than” signs that indicate the relationship between two adjacent cells. We solve this puzzle in the same way as other Latin Square puzzles, we eliminate the impossible by marking in a cell the numbers that can’t go there.
4 2
We use the same notation as we did for puzzle 7
  • If the cell can’t contain a 1, make a mark in the top-left quadrant.
  • If the cell can’t contain a 2, make a mark in the top-right quadrant.
  • If the cell can’t contain a 3, make a mark in the bottom-left quadrant.
  • If the cell can’t contain a 4, make a mark in the bottom-right quadrant.
  • If the cell can’t contain a 5, make a mark in the middle.

Puzzle 8 step 2

We now turn our attention to the “less than” and “greater than” signs. A sign indicates the relationship between two cells: the greater cell and the lesser cell. Each cell has a minimum possible number and a maximum possible number.
  • For an empty cell, the minimum is 1 and the maximum is 5.
  • For an numbered cell, the minimum and the maximum are that number.
  • For a marked cell, the minimum and maximum depend on which marks are in the cell. For example,
    • If a cell has only a 1-mark. Its minimum is 2 but its maximum is still 5
    • If a cell has a 4-mark and a 5-mark. Its minimum is still 1 but its maximum is 3
    • If a cell has only a 3-mark. Its minimum is still 1 and its maximum is still 5
We can eliminate impossibilities because:
  • The greater cell can’t be any number from 1 to the minimum of the lesser cell.
  • The lesser cell can’t be any number from maximum of the greater cell to 5.
For example:
  • If both cells are empty, the lesser can’t be 5, the greater can’t be 1.
  • If the lesser cell has a 1-mark, its minimum is 2, therefore the greater can’t be 1 or 2.
  • If the greater cell has a 5-mark, its maximum is 4, therefore the lesser can’t be 4 or 5.

Puzzle 8 step 4

We now appear to be stuck. There are no cells with four marks. There are no rows or columns where all but one have the same mark. But there is a row where all but two cells have the same two marks. On the second row, the middle and inside-right cells do not have a 1-mark and a 2-mark whereas the other three cells do have those marks. This means that the middle and inside-right cells must be 1 and 2 (but we don’t know which is which yet) so they can’t be 3, 4 or 5. We can, therefore, mark these cells as such.

Puzzle 8 step 7

There is now a column in which all but one of the cells has the same mark. And from this point we can finish the puzzle just by looking for two patterns:
  1. A cell with four marks
  2. A row or column where all-but-one cell have the same mark.
