Puzzle 5

Once upon a time, a king had a walled garden.
But he was fed up with walking into the dead ends, so he told his gardener to plant flowers in the dead ends so he wouldn't walk into them. So the gardener filled in all the dead ends with flower beds (which we represent using a hashed square)...
But the king declared, “There are still dead ends!” He had the gardener executed and hired a new gardener to finish the job. The new gardener filled in the new dead ends, but also filled in any dead ends that were created in the process.
Now the king could walk in the garden without walking into dead ends.

Lesson – the pleasure is in the doing

What the gardener did was take a maze (the king's walled garden) and eliminated the impossible, that is, filled in all the dead ends, and what remained was a clear path through the maze (the garden).
When solving a maze in a puzzle book, the idea is not to try to do it as quickly as possible and move on to the next puzzle. Solving puzzles is a pastime, not a race. When solving a puzzle, the pleasure comes from the repetitive, almost meditative, way we apply the simple rules and arrive at the solution. Nowhere is this illustrated more clearly than in solving a maze. Take a pencil, even a set of coloured pencils, and enjoy filling in the dead ends. Take your time, take pleasure in the doing. The fact that when all the dead ends are filled in you have “solved” the maze is secondary. But when you have done that you can take extra pleasure in traversing the maze along the clear path (or paths) you have created.
